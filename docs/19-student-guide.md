# Student Guide

For students using the desktop (or Android — see the note at the end) app to practice English.

## Logging in

Your teacher or school admin creates your account and gives you the email and initial password to log in with (see `docs/18-teacher-guide.md`). Logging in takes you straight to the conversation practice screen.

## Practicing conversation

1. Pick a scenario — Free Talk, Role Play, Interview Practice, Business English, Travel English, Daily Conversation, or Debate Practice — and start a new conversation. If your teacher gave your class an assignment, starting a conversation in the matching scenario counts toward completing it.
2. Type (or speak — see below) a message and send it. The AI partner replies in real time, adjusting its vocabulary and sentence complexity to a difficulty level estimated from your own past messages (so it gets more challenging as you improve, not by anyone manually setting a level).
3. If a message has a grammar mistake, you'll see the original alongside a suggested correction right under your message. Click **Explain** on any correction to get a short, step-by-step explanation of the rule involved, with an extra example — generated by the same local AI model, not a canned response.

## Vocabulary notebook

Words the AI thinks you found difficult (based on your conversations) get suggested for your notebook automatically after a conversation. You can also add any word manually. Each notebook entry includes a definition, an example sentence, synonyms/antonyms, and the word's own difficulty level (CEFR A1–C2) — generated once per word and reused for every student who looks it up, so it's instant after the first time.

## Pronunciation practice

A separate panel from regular conversation: type or pick a target phrase, click **Listen** to hear it spoken aloud by the system's text-to-speech voice, then **Record** yourself saying it. You'll get back what the system heard you say (via speech-to-text) and an accuracy score comparing it to the target phrase, plus brief feedback. This uses word-level comparison, not full phoneme-level pronunciation analysis — a reasonable first pass at "did you say roughly the right words," not a professional pronunciation coach.

## My progress

A panel showing your own activity over time: how many conversations/messages you've had, how often you've practiced recently, which grammar categories you make the most mistakes in, how your vocabulary notebook has grown, and your estimated overall CEFR level. This is the same view your teacher sees when they click your name in their roster — nothing hidden from you that they can see.

## Android

The Android app currently covers login and a connection-status check only; conversation, vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation practice are desktop-only in this release (tracked as a known gap in `docs/03-roadmap.md`'s backlog, not an oversight). Use the desktop app for the features described above.
